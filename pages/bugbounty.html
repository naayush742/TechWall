crowdsourced security · responsible disclosure · since 1995

Companies pay you to find their security flaws first.

A bug bounty program rewards independent security researchers — "ethical hackers" — for finding and responsibly reporting vulnerabilities, instead of those flaws being found (and exploited) by someone with worse intentions. This page covers where the idea came from, how a report actually moves through the system, where it's used today, and where to learn it properly.

01 / origin

From a Volkswagen Beetle to billion-dollar platforms

Paying outsiders to break things is older than computing itself — bug bounties just applied a very old idea to a very new problem.

The concept predates software entirely: in 1851, locksmith Alfred Charles Hobbs was paid handsomely to pick one of the era's strongest locks, publicly proving it could be beaten. In computing terms, the earliest known example came in 1983, when Hunter & Ready advertised "Get a bug if you find a bug" — rewarding bugs found in its VRTX operating system with an actual Volkswagen Beetle.

The first true bug bounty program, in the modern sense, launched on October 10, 1995: Netscape offered cash rewards (up to $1,000) for security flaws found in the beta of Netscape Navigator 2.0. The idea was championed internally by support engineer Jarrett Ridlinghafer, who is credited with coining the term "bug bounty" itself. Strangely, the idea didn't immediately spread — it took seven years before another major program appeared, when security firm iDefense launched its Vulnerability Contributor Program in 2002.

Mozilla launched its own program in 2004, and Google and Facebook followed in the years after, each running self-managed programs. The model truly scaled once dedicated platforms emerged to handle the logistics: HackerOne and Bugcrowd, both founded in 2012, made it possible for any company — not just tech giants with their own security teams — to run a bounty program. The model reached government in April 2016, when the US Department of Defense launched "Hack the Pentagon," resolving 138 unique vulnerabilities and proving the approach could work even for highly sensitive systems.

02 / mechanics

How a single bug report actually flows

From the moment a researcher spots something suspicious to the moment they get paid, every report moves through a fairly standard pipeline.

The reward usually scales with severity — a minor information leak might earn $50–$500, while a critical remote code execution bug on a major platform can pay out five or six figures.

◆ Scope

The exact set of domains, apps, or systems a program allows researchers to test — testing outside scope is unauthorized and often illegal.

◆ Severity rating

Bugs are typically scored using frameworks like CVSS, which determines how large the payout will be.

◆ Triage

A platform or internal team validates a report is real, not a duplicate, and accurately scoped before it reaches the vendor.

◆ Proof of concept (PoC)

A minimal, working demonstration that proves the vulnerability is real and exploitable, not just theoretical.

◆ Safe harbor

Legal language in a program's policy that protects good-faith researchers from prosecution for testing within scope.

◆ Vulnerability Disclosure Program (VDP)

A non-paid sibling to bounty programs — accepting reports and offering recognition, but no cash reward.

03 / applications

Who runs (and benefits from) bug bounties

What started as a browser-vendor experiment now spans nearly every industry that ships software.

01

Big tech companies

Google, Microsoft, Meta, and Apple all run large bounty programs, often paying six figures for the most critical vulnerabilities.

02

Governments

Following "Hack the Pentagon," the US military expanded into "Hack the Army" and "Hack the Air Force," and other governments have followed.

03

Startups and SaaS companies

Platforms like HackerOne and Bugcrowd let small teams run a credible security program without building an in-house red team.

04

Cryptocurrency and Web3 projects

Smart contract bugs can mean instant, irreversible loss of funds, making bounties (sometimes worth millions) a near-universal practice in crypto.

05

Independent security researchers

Many full-time bug hunters earn a living entirely from bounties — some make multiples of a typical local engineering salary.

06

Career entry point

Bug bounty platforms double as a public portfolio, helping researchers land security jobs by demonstrating real, validated findings.

Free, well-regarded places to actually learn it
