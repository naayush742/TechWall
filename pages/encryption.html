cryptography · symmetric & asymmetric · since 100 BC

Turning meaning into noise — and back again, only for the right key.

Encryption is the practice of scrambling information so only someone holding the correct key can read it. It's quietly running underneath almost every digital interaction you have — this page covers where the idea came from, how the math actually works, where it's used today, and where to learn it properly.

01 / origin

From shaved heads to silicon chips

Encryption is older than computers by roughly two thousand years — only the medium has changed.

The earliest documented technique isn't even encryption by modern definition — it's steganography: the Greek historian Herodotus describes a message tattooed onto a servant's shaved scalp, hidden once the hair regrew. The first true cipher most people learn is the Caesar cipher, used by Julius Caesar around 100–44 BC, which simply shifts each letter a fixed number of places down the alphabet. It held up for centuries until the 9th-century Arab mathematician Al-Kindi broke it using frequency analysis — counting how often each letter appears in a language to reverse-engineer the shift.

The 20th century turned cryptography into mechanical engineering. In 1918, German engineer Arthur Scherbius built the Enigma machine, a rotor-based device the German military relied on heavily through WWII. Polish mathematician Marian Rejewski first broke Enigma in 1932, and the effort was later scaled up at Bletchley Park by British codebreakers including Alan Turing — a campaign widely credited with shortening the war and seeding the foundations of modern computing.

The digital era split encryption into two parallel revolutions. In the 1970s, IBM developed a cipher that became the Data Encryption Standard (DES), adopted as a US federal standard in 1976/77 — the first encryption standard built for computers rather than humans. Then in 1976, Whitfield Diffie and Martin Hellman published the idea of public-key cryptography: a way to securely exchange a secret without ever having met. A year later, Rivest, Shamir, and Adleman turned that idea into a working algorithm — RSA, still in everyday use today. By 2001, DES's short 56-bit key had become breakable, and it was formally replaced by the Advanced Encryption Standard (AES), the symmetric cipher that still secures most of the internet.

02 / mechanics

Two families of encryption — and how they team up

Nearly everything in modern cryptography is one of two approaches, often combined: one fast secret-sharing method, and one clever trick for exchanging that secret safely in the first place.

Symmetric ciphers are fast but need a pre-shared secret. Asymmetric ciphers solve the sharing problem but are slow. Real systems like HTTPS/TLS use asymmetric encryption only to exchange a key, then switch to symmetric encryption for speed.

◆ Plaintext

The original, readable message or data before any transformation is applied.

◆ Ciphertext

The scrambled, unreadable output produced once an encryption algorithm and key are applied.

◆ Key

The secret value that controls exactly how the scrambling happens — and how it's reversed.

◆ Algorithm / cipher

The mathematical procedure itself — AES, RSA, ChaCha20 — that's publicly known and trusted to be secure.

◆ Hashing (related, not encryption)

A one-way fingerprint of data — used for passwords and integrity checks, never meant to be reversed.

◆ End-to-end encryption

Data is encrypted on the sender's device and only decrypted on the recipient's — not even the service provider in between can read it.

03 / applications

Where encryption is quietly working right now

It's invisible by design — most people interact with strong encryption dozens of times a day without noticing.

01

Web browsing (HTTPS/TLS)

The padlock icon in your browser means traffic between you and the site is encrypted in transit, combining asymmetric key exchange with symmetric bulk encryption.

02

Messaging apps

End-to-end encryption in apps like Signal and WhatsApp ensures only the sender and recipient can read messages, not the platform itself.

03

Banking and payments

Card transactions, online banking sessions, and crypto-wallet signatures all rely on layered encryption to prevent interception and fraud.

04

Disk and device encryption

Tools like BitLocker and FileVault encrypt an entire hard drive, so a stolen laptop's data stays unreadable without the login key.

05

Password storage

Services store a salted hash of your password instead of the password itself, so even a database breach doesn't expose your actual credentials.

06

VPNs and corporate networks

VPN tunnels encrypt all traffic between a device and a remote server, hiding browsing activity from local networks and ISPs.
