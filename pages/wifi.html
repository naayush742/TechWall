History

🕰️

WiFi is based on the IEEE 802.11 standards, first published in 1997 covering 2.4 GHz operation at 2 Mbps. The WiFi Alliance was formed in 1999 and trademarked 'Wi-Fi'. The landmark 802.11b standard (1999) offered 11 Mbps and drove consumer adoption. Successive generations: 802.11a/g (54 Mbps, 2003), 802.11n / WiFi 4 (600 Mbps, 2009), 802.11ac / WiFi 5 (3.5 Gbps, 2013), 802.11ax / WiFi 6 (9.6 Gbps, 2019), and WiFi 7 (46 Gbps, 2024).

Why We Use WiFi

WiFi enables the wireless internet access that powers smartphones, laptops, smart home devices, IoT sensors, security cameras, and industrial automation. Modern WiFi 6 addresses dense environments (apartments, stadiums, offices) with OFDMA (multiple users simultaneously) and Target Wake Time for battery-efficient IoT devices.

Key Facts & Features

  • 🧠 2.4 GHz band
    longer range, slower, more interference; 5 GHz: shorter range, faster
  • 🎯 WPA3 is the
    WPA3 is the current security standard (replaced WPA2 in 2018)
  • ⚡ SSID
    the human-readable name of a WiFi network
  • ⚠️ OFDMA (WiFi 6+)
    allows one access point to serve multiple devices simultaneously
  • 📌 MU-MIMO
    multiple input/multiple output antennas for parallel streams
  • 💡 WiFi 7 (802.11be, 2024)
    46 Gbps theoretical maximum with 320 MHz channels
