01 — Origin

From "BackRub" to a garage, to the world's most-used website

The whole company traces back to a PhD dissertation topic that almost wasn't chosen.

In 1995, Larry Page, a computer science PhD student at Stanford, was searching for a dissertation topic and — on his advisor's encouragement — chose to study the link structure of the World Wide Web as a giant graph. Fellow Stanford student Sergey Brin joined his research project, nicknamed "BackRub," which began crawling the web in March 1996. The pair, along with early collaborator Scott Hassan, developed the PageRank algorithm — instead of simply counting keyword matches, it treated each link to a page as a "vote" for that page's importance, producing dramatically more relevant results than existing search engines.

Their 1998 paper, "The Anatomy of a Large-Scale Hypertextual Web Search Engine," became one of the most downloaded academic papers in history. The growing project strained Stanford's infrastructure, so Page and Brin raised about $1 million from family, friends, and investors — including an early $100,000 check from Sun Microsystems co-founder Andy Bechtolsheim, written before the company even legally existed. On September 4, 1998, Page and Brin formally incorporated Google Inc. in a friend's garage in Menlo Park, California. The name was a deliberate misspelling of "googol" — the number 1 followed by 100 zeros — reflecting their ambition to organize an enormous amount of information.

Growth was explosive: by the end of 1998 Google had indexed roughly 60 million pages; within a year it was processing 500,000 queries a day. The company moved to Mountain View, California in 2003, the location of its "Googleplex" headquarters ever since. Google went public in August 2004. Major expansions followed quickly — Gmail (2004), Google Maps (2005), the $1.65 billion acquisition of YouTube (2006), and Google Chrome (2008). In 2015, Google reorganized under a new holding company, Alphabet Inc., separating its core internet products from more experimental "Other Bets" ventures. Page and Brin stepped back from day-to-day leadership in December 2019, handing the CEO role to Sundar Pichai.

02 — Mechanics

The core idea: links as votes

PageRank's breakthrough was treating the web itself as a kind of democracy — a page is important if other important pages link to it.

PageRank recursively weighs each link by the importance of the page sending it — a link from a well-linked page counts more than a link from an obscure one, which is what made early Google results dramatically more relevant than competitors.

◆ Crawling

Automated bots ("spiders") continuously discover new and updated pages across the web by following links.

◆ Indexing

Crawled content is processed and stored in a massive searchable database, organized by the words and topics it contains.

◆ PageRank

The original link-based authority algorithm — still one ingredient among hundreds in how modern Google ranks results.

◆ Ranking signals

Today's algorithm combines hundreds of factors beyond links — relevance, freshness, page speed, and more.

◆ Knowledge Graph

A structured database of real-world entities and their relationships, powering direct-answer info boxes in search results.

◆ Ads auction

Google's primary revenue engine — advertisers bid in real time for ad placement alongside organic search results.

03 — Applications

What Google actually is today

"Google" stopped meaning just a search box a long time ago — it's now a sprawling ecosystem spanning nearly every category of consumer and enterprise technology.

Usecase 01

Search & Information

Google Search remains the company's core product and the dominant way most of the world finds information online.

Usecase 02

Advertising

Google Ads and its ad-auction systems form the company's primary revenue engine, powering free consumer software globally.

Usecase 03

Productivity & Mail

Gmail, Docs, Sheets, Drive, and Calendar form Google Workspace, hosting billions of individual and business workflows daily.

Usecase 04

Mobile & Hardware

Android powers the vast majority of smartphones globally, supported by hardware ecosystems like Pixel phones.

Usecase 05

Cloud Computing

Google Cloud Platform (GCP) provides enterprise hosting, database administration, and container nodes globally.

Usecase 06

Artificial Intelligence

Through DeepMind, Google conducts research on the Gemini model family, creating agentic architectures and tools.
